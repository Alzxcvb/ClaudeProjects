Limits to Growth & Polycrisis

Graduate thesis research on systemic constraints, interlinked crises, and frameworks for understanding the limits of global growth

Research Question

How do systemic constraints and interlinked crises (polycrises) limit global growth, and what frameworks can help understand, predict, or mitigate these limits?

This research combines quantitative system dynamics modeling with qualitative case studies to examine ecological, economic, and social feedback loops that constrain growth — and proposes actionable frameworks for resilience.

Key Literature

A curated review of foundational and modern research, from the original 1972 MIT study through 2024 polycrisis frameworks.

Foundational Studies (1972–2004)

1972

The Limits to Growth

Donella Meadows, Dennis Meadows, Jorgen Randers, William Behrens III

Universe Books / Club of Rome

Using the World3 system dynamics model, projected that business-as-usual growth trends would hit planetary limits within 100 years, causing decline in population and industrial capacity. The "stabilized world" scenario showed equilibrium was possible with policy changes. Sold 30+ million copies in 30 languages.

Club of Rome → · Full Text (Dartmouth) →

1992

Beyond the Limits

Donella Meadows, Dennis Meadows, Jorgen Randers

Chelsea Green Publishing

20-year update concluding humanity had already "overshot" several critical limits by 1992. Updated World3 with new data; original projections remained broadly on track. The window for smooth transition was narrowing.

Open Library →

2004

Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update

Donella Meadows, Dennis Meadows, Jorgen Randers

Chelsea Green Publishing

Found global trends closely tracked the original BAU scenario. Ecological footprint data showed humanity using ~20% more resources annually than Earth could regenerate. Political will for systemic change had not materialized.

Chelsea Green →

The World3 Model — Key Scenarios

Scenario Assumptions Outcome
BAU (Business as Usual) No policy changes, historical trends continue Collapse of industrial output and food by mid-21st century via resource depletion + pollution
BAU2 Doubled nonrenewable resources Collapse delayed but still occurs, driven primarily by pollution
CT (Comprehensive Technology) Aggressive tech solutions applied Collapse further delayed but eventual decline from land erosion, food shortages, pollution
SW (Stabilized World) Tech + social policy (population stabilization, equity, reduced consumption) Only scenario that avoids collapse and achieves equilibrium

Modern Validation & Extensions (2008–2024)

2021

Update to Limits to Growth: Comparing the World3 Model with Empirical Data

Gaya Herrington

Journal of Industrial Ecology (Yale), Vol. 25, Issue 3

Landmark study comparing 48 years of empirical data (1972–2020) against World3 scenarios. Data best fits BAU2 and CT scenarios — both project growth halt by ~2040. The SW path remains technically possible, making the 2020s the decisive decade. Conducted as Harvard master's thesis; Herrington was a KPMG Director at the time.

DOI: 10.1111/jiec.13084 →

2008–2014

LtG Empirical Validation Series

Graham Turner

Global Environmental Change (2008); MSSI Research Paper (2014)

First systematic comparison of 30 then 40 years of real data to World3. Found alarming fidelity to BAU standard run. 2014 paper concluded data was consistent with the onset of global collapse in the early-to-mid 21st century. Direct intellectual predecessor to Herrington's work.

2008 Paper (DOI) → · 2014 Paper (PDF) → · ResearchGate →

2022

Earth4All: A Survival Guide for Humanity

Dixson-Decleve, Gaffney, Ghosh, Randers, Rockstrom, Stoknes

New Society Publishers / Club of Rome

Spiritual successor to LtG, 50 years later. New system dynamics model identifies five turnarounds: ending poverty, addressing inequality, empowering women, transforming food systems, transforming energy. "Too Little Too Late" vs "Giant Leap" scenarios. Co-authored by original LtG author Jorgen Randers.

Earth4All → · Club of Rome →

2024

What is a Global Polycrisis?

Michael Lawrence, Thomas Homer-Dixon, et al.

Global Sustainability, Cambridge University Press

Formalizes the polycrisis framework — simultaneous interacting crises (climate, energy, food, financial, geopolitical) that compound risk. Explicitly validates LtG's feedback-loop approach as predicting exactly this cascading dynamic. From the Cascade Institute.

DOI: 10.1017/sus.2024.1 →

2019

Integrated Global Model for Resource and Population Sustainability

Pasqualino, Monasterolo, Jones

Sustainability, Vol. 11(14), 3995

Built on World3 framework incorporating financial system dynamics. Found that resource constraints and population dynamics interact with financial instability to accelerate decline, adding finance as a collapse-risk amplifier.

DOI: 10.3390/su11143995 →

2011

The Limits to Growth Revisited

Ugo Bardi

Springer

Rigorous defense and re-analysis of LtG methodology. Demonstrated that most criticisms were based on misreadings — the study never "predicted" specific dates for resource exhaustion. Connected LtG to modern collapse dynamics ("Seneca Effect").

DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4419-9416-5 →

2012

2052: A Global Forecast for the Next Forty Years

Jorgen Randers

Chelsea Green / Club of Rome

Original LtG co-author's personal 40-year forecast. Projects slower economic growth, +2.8C by 2052, increasing inequality, and eventual welfare decline — consistent with BAU2/CT trajectories. Argues democratic systems too slow for long-term environmental threats.

Chelsea Green → · Club of Rome →

Key Findings Across the Literature

  1. The 1972 model has held up remarkably well. Multiple independent validation studies show 50 years of empirical data tracking the BAU family of scenarios closely.
  2. The decisive period is now. The 2020s–2030s are the critical window — either managed transition or decline.
  3. Technology alone is insufficient. The CT scenario still results in decline. Only the SW scenario, combining technology with deliberate social choices, avoids it.
  4. "Collapse" means sustained decline, not apocalypse. Gradual reduction in industrial output, food production, and services per capita — a long decline, not sudden catastrophe.
  5. The polycrisis literature validates the systems approach. Interacting crises behave in exactly the feedback-loop-driven way that World3 modeled.

Methodology (In Development)

The thesis is converging on a polycrisis + systems modeling approach — more novel and globally applicable than purely ecological limits modeling.

Approaches Under Consideration

System Dynamics Modeling

Stella, Vensim, or MATLAB — extending World3-style feedback loop modeling with updated data and new subsystems

Network Theory

Modeling cascading crises as network failures — nodes (systems) and edges (interdependencies) that propagate shocks

Scenario Analysis

"What if" modeling under different assumptions of resource depletion, climate trajectories, and social unrest

Mixed Methods

Quantitative system dynamics + qualitative historical case studies (Great Depression, 1970s oil shocks, COVID-19 + supply chain collapse)
