Sources & Evidence

Peer-Reviewed Research Behind This Program

Every training decision in this program is backed by peer-reviewed research published in indexed medical and sports science journals. No Instagram science. No bro-science. No fads. Click any [n] citation in the program to find the supporting study here.

  1. "Effects of Resistance Training Frequency on Measures of Muscle Hypertrophy: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis."
    Schoenfeld BJ, Ogborn D, Krieger JW. Sports Med. 2016;46(11):1689-1697.
    Training muscles 2x/week produces superior hypertrophy vs 1x/week. This is why we use a 6-day PPL split hitting each muscle twice.
    PubMed →
  2. "Dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass: A systematic review and meta-analysis."
    Schoenfeld BJ, Ogborn D, Krieger JW. J Sports Sci. 2017;35(11):1073-1082.
    10+ sets per muscle group per week optimizes hypertrophy. Our program delivers 10-16 sets per muscle group weekly.
    PubMed →
  3. "Loading Recommendations for Muscle Strength, Hypertrophy, and Local Endurance: A Re-Examination of the Repetition Continuum."
    Schoenfeld BJ, Grgic J, Van Every DW, Plotkin DL. Sports (Basel). 2021;9(2):32.
    Hypertrophy occurs across 6-30 reps when training near failure. Our 8-12 rep range for compounds and 12-15 for accessories is well within this window.
    PubMed →
  4. "Progression Models in Resistance Training for Healthy Adults."
    American College of Sports Medicine. Med Sci Sports Exerc. 2009;41(3):687-708.
    Progressive overload (systematically increasing weight/volume) is required for continued adaptation. Our 3-phase progression scheme follows ACSM guidelines.
    PubMed →
  5. "A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength in healthy adults."
    Morton RW, Murphy KT, McKellar SR, et al. Br J Sports Med. 2018;52(6):376-384.
    1.6g/kg/day protein is sufficient to maximize muscle gains. At 58kg, that's ~93g/day minimum. Alex already exceeds this.
    PubMed →
  6. "Is an Energy Surplus Required to Maximize Skeletal Muscle Hypertrophy Associated With Resistance Training."
    Slater GJ, Dieter BP, Marsh DJ, et al. Front Nutr. 2019;6:131.
    A caloric surplus of 350-500 kcal/day optimizes muscle gain while limiting fat accumulation. Our +400-500 cal target is based on this.
    PubMed →
  7. "Post-exercise cold water immersion attenuates acute anabolic signalling and long-term adaptations in muscle to strength training."
    Roberts LA, Raastad T, Markworth JF, et al. J Physiol. 2015;593(18):4285-4301.
    Cold water immersion immediately after strength training reduces muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy. This is why we say do NOT cold plunge right after lifting.
    PubMed →
  8. "Association Between Sauna Bathing and Fatal Cardiovascular and All-Cause Mortality Events."
    Laukkanen T, Khan H, Zaccardi F, Laukkanen JA. JAMA Intern Med. 2015;175(4):542-548.
    2-3 sauna sessions/week reduced cardiovascular mortality risk by 27% vs 1x/week in a 20-year cohort of 2,315 men.
    PubMed →
  9. "Cardiovascular and Other Health Benefits of Sauna Bathing: A Review of the Evidence."
    Laukkanen JA, Laukkanen T, Kunutsor SK. Mayo Clin Proc. 2018;93(8):1111-1121.
    Regular sauna bathing improves endothelial function, reduces arterial stiffness, modulates the autonomic nervous system, and lowers blood pressure.
    PubMed →
  10. "Sleep and muscle recovery: endocrinological and molecular basis for a new and promising hypothesis."
    Dattilo M, Antunes HK, Medeiros A, et al. Med Hypotheses. 2011;77(2):220-222.
    Sleep deprivation increases cortisol and decreases testosterone and IGF-1, creating a proteolytic (muscle-wasting) environment. 7-9 hours is the evidence-based target.
    PubMed →

What This Program Does NOT Use

  • Social media fitness influencer advice
  • Proprietary "systems" without published evidence
  • Supplements beyond protein (which has strong evidence)
  • Exercises or protocols without established safety profiles
  • Anecdotal "what worked for me" reasoning
