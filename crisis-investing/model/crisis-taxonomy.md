# Crisis Taxonomy & Asset Allocation Framework

**Purpose:** Classify economic crises by type, then map optimal asset allocations to each type. The central insight is that crisis identification is the primary decision — asset selection follows from the classification.

---

## The Five Crisis Types

### Type 1: Inflationary / Stagflationary Crisis

**Definition:** Sustained rise in price levels combined with economic stagnation. Central banks lose control of inflation, often due to supply shocks, excessive money printing, or commodity disruptions.

**Historical Examples:**
- 1970s US Stagflation (oil shocks + monetary expansion)
- Weimar Germany hyperinflation (1921-23)
- Post-WWI European inflation broadly
- Potential: 2025-26 tariff-driven inflation?

**Mechanism:** Currency purchasing power erodes. Nominal prices rise across the board. Real interest rates go deeply negative. Fixed-income instruments lose real value. Real assets gain because they're priced in inflating currency.

**Optimal Allocation:**
| Asset | Allocation | Rationale |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Gold / precious metals | 25-35% | Historical gold standard for inflation — 2,000% gain in 1970s |
| Commodities (broad basket) | 15-20% | Commodities drive 47% of headline inflation effects; direct hedge |
| Farmland / agricultural REITs | 15-20% | Productive real asset, uncorrelated to equities |
| Commodity-producer equities | 10-15% | Oil, mining, agriculture companies benefit from rising commodity prices |
| TIPS / inflation-linked bonds | 5-10% | Direct inflation linkage, though limited in hyperinflation |
| Cash / short-duration bonds | 5-10% | Minimal — cash is the asset being destroyed |

**Avoid:** Long-duration bonds, fixed-rate debt instruments, growth equities with no pricing power, cash hoarding.

---

### Type 2: Deflationary Panic

**Definition:** Acute demand destruction, credit contraction, and asset price collapse. Often triggered by financial system failures, over-leverage, or sudden confidence loss.

**Historical Examples:**
- Great Depression (1929-33)
- 2008 Global Financial Crisis (acute phase: Sep 2008 - Mar 2009)
- 1907 Panic
- 1997 Asian Financial Crisis

**Mechanism:** Credit contracts. Asset prices fall across the board as leveraged positions unwind. Cash becomes king because deflation increases its purchasing power. Commodities fall with demand. Even "safe" assets like gold can initially decline as forced selling hits all markets.

**Optimal Allocation:**
| Asset | Allocation | Rationale |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Reserve-currency sovereign bonds (US Treasuries) | 35-45% | Flight-to-safety asset; negative beta to equities in 2008 |
| Cash (reserve currency) | 20-25% | Deflation increases purchasing power; dry powder for distressed assets |
| Gold | 10-15% | Underperforms initially but recovers; insurance position |
| Defensive equities (staples, healthcare) | 5-10% | Milder drawdowns than cyclicals (~30% vs 50%+) |
| Distressed debt / deep value | 5-10% | Deploying cash into forced-sale opportunities |

**Avoid:** Commodities (pro-cyclical), leveraged anything, cyclical equities, non-reserve-currency debt, illiquid assets.

---

### Type 3: Currency Collapse / Hyperinflation

**Definition:** Complete loss of confidence in a sovereign currency. Prices rise by orders of magnitude. The currency ceases to function as a medium of exchange.

**Historical Examples:**
- Soviet Union collapse (1991-92) — ruble inflation 2,300% in 1992
- Venezuela (2016-present) — ongoing hyperinflation
- Zimbabwe (2007-09) — peak inflation 79.6 billion % per month
- French Assignat collapse (1789-96)
- Weimar Germany (extreme version of Type 1)

**Mechanism:** The currency itself becomes worthless. All assets denominated in the collapsing currency go to zero in real terms. The economy reverts to barter or adopts a foreign currency as de facto legal tender. Only assets with value independent of the failing currency preserve wealth.

**Optimal Allocation:**
| Asset | Allocation | Rationale |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Foreign hard currency (USD, CHF, EUR) | 30-40% | De facto replacement currency in every modern hyperinflation |
| Gold / physical precious metals | 25-30% | No counterparty risk, universally liquid, 2,000+ year record |
| Productive real assets (land, property) | 15-20% | Utility-based value independent of currency |
| Commodity stockpiles / barter goods | 5-10% | Direct exchange value when monetary system fails |
| Foreign-denominated equities | 5-10% | Companies earning in stable currencies |

**Avoid:** Anything denominated in the collapsing currency — bonds, cash, bank deposits, domestic fixed-income.

**Key distinction from Type 1:** Type 1 is currency *erosion* (5-20% annually). Type 3 is currency *death* (100%+ monthly). The allocation shift is dramatic — in Type 1 you still use the domestic currency; in Type 3 you abandon it entirely.

### Type 4: Speculative Bubble Pop

**Definition:** Collapse of an asset class that has been driven to unsustainable valuations by speculative excess. The broader economy may or may not be directly affected.

**Historical Examples:**
- Dutch Tulip Bubble (1637) — rare bulbs fell ~99%
- South Sea Bubble (1720) — shares fell from £950 to £124 (87% decline)
- Dot-com crash (2000) — NASDAQ fell 78%
- US Housing Bubble (2006-08) — triggered broader crisis (became Type 2)
- Japan asset bubble (1989) — Nikkei still hasn't recovered after 35 years

**Mechanism:** A specific asset class is bid up far beyond fundamental value. When confidence breaks, that asset collapses. Contagion to the broader economy depends on how much leverage is connected to the bubble asset. If leverage is contained (tulips), damage is limited. If leverage is systemic (2008 housing), the bubble pop triggers a Type 2 deflationary panic.

**Optimal Allocation:**
| Asset | Allocation | Rationale |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Cash / money market | 30-40% | Dry powder to buy distressed assets post-crash |
| Established enterprises with real cash flow | 20-25% | VOC survived tulip bubble; Bank of England survived South Sea |
| Gold | 10-15% | Modest hedge; not the primary play here |
| Non-bubble real assets | 10-15% | Diversification away from the inflated sector |
| Short positions on bubble asset | 5-10% | Direct profit from the collapse (timing risk is extreme) |

**Avoid:** The bubble asset (obviously), anything leveraged to it, anything in the same "story" (e.g., in 2000, even profitable tech companies fell).

**Critical insight:** The hardest part of a bubble isn't the collapse — it's identifying it before the pop. "The market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent." (Keynes)

---

### Type 5: Regime / State Collapse

**Definition:** The governing political system disintegrates. Legal frameworks, property rights, trade networks, and monetary systems break down simultaneously.

**Historical Examples:**
- Fall of Western Roman Empire (~476 AD)
- Soviet Union dissolution (1991)
- French Revolution (1789) — overlaps with Type 3
- Ottoman Empire dissolution (post-WWI)
- Yugoslavia breakup (1990s)

**Mechanism:** The state ceases to function. Government debt becomes worthless. Property rights depend on the ability to physically defend them. Trade networks collapse to local scope. Currency issued by the failed state is abandoned. New power structures emerge — often based on land control, military power, or control of essential resources.

**Optimal Allocation:**
| Asset | Allocation | Rationale |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Gold / precious metals (physical, portable) | 25-30% | No counterparty, survives regime change — Solidus lasted 700 years |
| Productive land (defensible) | 20-25% | Basis of feudalism post-Rome; food + shelter + security |
| Foreign assets (outside collapsing jurisdiction) | 20-25% | Geographic diversification is survival |
| Portable skills and social capital | Non-financial | In every collapse, human capital outlasted financial capital |
| Barter commodities / essentials | 5-10% | Immediate survival utility |

**Avoid:** Government debt of the failing state, urban real estate in trade-dependent cities, any asset requiring the state's legal system for enforcement, complex financial instruments.

**Key distinction from other types:** This is not a financial crisis — it's a civilizational one. Financial optimization is secondary to physical security and survival utility. The "portfolio" concept itself may not apply in the acute phase.

## Cross-Crisis Decision Matrix

### How to Use This Framework

**Step 1:** Assess the current environment against crisis-type indicators
**Step 2:** Assign probabilities to each crisis type
**Step 3:** Blend allocations weighted by probability
**Step 4:** Maintain minimum positions in each crisis type for tail-risk protection

### Leading Indicators by Crisis Type

| Crisis Type | Leading Indicators |
|-------------|-------------------|
| **Type 1: Inflation/Stagflation** | Rising CPI, negative real rates, commodity price surges, supply chain disruptions, wage-price spirals, central bank behind the curve |
| **Type 2: Deflationary Panic** | Credit spread widening, yield curve inversion, falling money velocity, bank stress, rising default rates, VIX spike |
| **Type 3: Currency Collapse** | Accelerating money supply growth, loss of foreign reserves, capital flight, black market FX premiums, political instability |
| **Type 4: Bubble Pop** | P/E ratios >2 standard deviations above mean, retail investor mania, excessive leverage in one sector, "this time is different" narratives |
| **Type 5: Regime Collapse** | State unable to collect taxes, military fragmentation, loss of territorial control, simultaneous economic + political + social crises |

### The Ambiguity Problem

Many crises start as one type and evolve into another:
- 2008 started as Type 4 (housing bubble) and became Type 2 (deflationary panic)
- Weimar started as Type 1 (inflation) and became Type 3 (currency collapse)
- Soviet collapse was simultaneously Type 3 and Type 5

**The model must account for crisis evolution.** A static allocation set at crisis onset may become wrong as the crisis mutates. The recommendation is to:

1. Maintain a **base allocation** that hedges across all types (permanent tail-risk sleeve)
2. Add a **tactical overlay** that shifts based on real-time indicator readings
3. Set **trigger points** for allocation shifts as one crisis type evolves into another

## Sample Portfolio: All-Weather Crisis Hedge

For a permanent tail-risk sleeve that doesn't try to predict crisis type:

| Asset | Allocation | Covers |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Gold / precious metals | 20% | Types 1, 3, 5 |
| Reserve-currency sovereign bonds | 20% | Type 2 |
| Farmland / agricultural exposure | 15% | Types 1, 5 |
| Foreign hard currency basket | 15% | Type 3 |
| Cash (reserve currency) | 15% | Types 2, 4 |
| Commodity-producer equities | 10% | Type 1 |
| Defensive equities | 5% | Moderate cushion across types |

**Expected behavior:** This portfolio will underperform in normal times (opportunity cost of holding gold, cash, and farmland instead of equities). It is designed to preserve capital during crises, not to maximize returns during stability.

**Trade-off:** ~2-4% annual opportunity cost vs. equities in normal markets, in exchange for significant drawdown protection during crises.

## Open Questions for Model Refinement

1. **Crypto as digital gold?** Bitcoin has limited crisis data (only existed since 2009). Behaved as risk-on asset in 2020/2022, not safe haven. Too early to include with confidence.
2. **Options-based tail hedging?** Deep OTM puts provide crisis protection with defined cost. Not covered in historical analysis but relevant for modern implementation.
3. **Rebalancing rules?** How frequently should the tactical overlay be reassessed? Monthly? Quarterly? Event-driven?
4. **Benchmark selection?** What should this portfolio be benchmarked against — 60/40? Pure equity? An endowment model?
