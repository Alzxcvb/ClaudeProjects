# Historical Crisis Asset Performance Matrix

Complete case studies for 10 major economic crises. For each: context, what lost value, what held value, best practical investment, and key quantitative data.

---

## PRIMARY CASE STUDIES

---

### 1. Dutch Tulip Bubble (1637)

**Context:** During the Dutch Golden Age, speculative trading in rare tulip bulbs drove prices to extraordinary levels. Between December 1636 and February 1637, rare "broken" (variegated) bulbs appreciated 12x. At peak, a single Semper Augustus bulb sold for 5,000 guilders — the price of a fine Amsterdam house.

**Crisis Type:** Speculative Bubble Pop (Type 4)

**What Lost Value:**
- Rare tulip bulbs — crashed ~99% from peak prices
- Futures contracts on tulips — became unenforceable; buyers simply walked away

**What Held Value:**
- **Dutch East India Company (VOC) shares** — fundamentals remained sound; VOC continued expanding and eventually became the most valuable company in history (estimated $7.9 trillion in modern terms)
- **Land and real estate** — property values remained stable throughout
- **Trade goods** — the Dutch Republic's commerce in spices, textiles, and global trade was completely unaffected
- **Gold and precious metals** — maintained value as traditional stores

**Best Practical Investment:** VOC shares or land. The tulip bubble was remarkably contained — only ~350 people were actively involved in speculative tulip trading.

**Key Revisionist Finding:** Modern scholarship (Anne Goldgar) has shown the tulip bubble's economic impact was dramatically overstated. Fewer than half a dozen people experienced documented financial trouble. The Dutch Republic remained one of the world's leading economies with the highest per capita income from 1600-1720. The "lesson" is less about bubbles destroying economies and more about how isolated speculation doesn't necessarily create systemic risk — unless leverage connects it to the broader financial system.

### 2. South Sea Bubble (1720)

**Context:** The South Sea Company was granted a monopoly on British trade with South America and took on the national debt in exchange for government bond conversions. Shares were bid up from ~£128 to over £950 on speculative mania, then collapsed.

**Crisis Type:** Speculative Bubble Pop (Type 4), with systemic contagion

**What Lost Value:**
- **South Sea Company shares** — from £950 to £124 (87% decline)
- Personal fortunes of investors — bankruptcies and suicides followed the crash
- Public trust in financial markets — led to the Bubble Act restricting joint-stock companies for over a century

**What Held Value:**
- **Bank of England stock** — the Bank stepped in to stabilize markets and manage the fallout; its institutional credibility was reinforced
- **Government bonds (restructured)** — Robert Walpole restructured the national debt into the Bank of England, Treasury, and a new Sinking Fund. The restructured instruments survived
- **Confiscated land** — Parliament seized South Sea directors' estates and redistributed them to victims; the land itself retained full value
- **Established merchant enterprises** — businesses with genuine trade operations and cash flow were unaffected

**Best Practical Investment:** Bank of England shares, or cash to deploy into distressed assets after the crash. Holding government debt through the restructuring also worked, though with interim pain.

**Key Insight:** Institutional credibility mattered more than individual security prices. The crisis strengthened the Bank of England and led to better financial regulation. The companies that survived were those with real operations, not paper value.

### 3. Fall of the Western Roman Empire (~200-476 AD)

**Context:** A multi-century decline driven by currency debasement, military overstretch, barbarian invasions, political fragmentation, and economic breakdown. Not a single event but a gradual system failure.

**Crisis Type:** Regime/State Collapse (Type 5) combined with Currency Collapse (Type 3)

**What Lost Value Catastrophically:**
- **Silver coinage (Denarius)** — debased from 4.5g pure silver to 0.5% silver content by 265 AD. Prices rose 1,000% across the empire
- **Long-distance trade networks** — international commerce collapsed due to piracy, insecurity, and infrastructure decay
- **Urban real estate** — cities were abandoned as trade and government functions failed
- **Government obligations** — worthless as the state fragmented
- **All debased currency holdings** — by 330 AD, one gold Solidus was worth hundreds of thousands of Denarii

**What Held Value:**
- **Gold (especially the Solidus coin)** — Constantine's Solidus (4.5g pure gold, introduced ~312 AD, struck 72 to the Roman pound) remained essentially unaltered in weight, dimensions, and purity for 700 years. It was the only trusted medium of exchange during monetary collapse
- **Productive agricultural land** — became the fundamental store of value and basis of the emerging feudal system. Hundreds of thousands of Romans fled cities for rural holdings
- **Military capability** — private militias and the ability to provide physical protection became a direct form of wealth
- **Older, purer coins** — hoarded as their metal content exceeded face value
- **Barter goods** — communities reverted to direct exchange as monetary systems failed

**Best Practical Investment (for someone in ~400 AD):**
1. Convert all liquid assets to gold Solidi
2. Acquire defensible agricultural land with water access
3. Move family out of vulnerable urban centers
4. Establish relationships with local military leaders or build a retinue

**Key Insight:** The transition to feudalism wasn't irrational — it was an optimal survival strategy. When the state cannot provide security, trade networks, or monetary stability, the only viable "investment" is self-sufficient productive capacity (land) protected by physical force (military retainers). Financial assets require institutional infrastructure that no longer existed.

### 4. Collapse of the Soviet Union (1991)

**Context:** The USSR dissolved in December 1991 after years of economic stagnation, failed reforms (perestroika), and political upheaval. The transition from command economy to market economy was chaotic and devastating for ordinary citizens.

**Crisis Type:** Regime/State Collapse (Type 5) + Currency Collapse (Type 3)

**What Lost Value Catastrophically:**
- **Ruble savings and bank deposits** — 1991 inflation: 96% (officially), ~200% (reality). 1992 inflation: 2,300%. Lifetime savings evaporated
- **Ruble-denominated government bonds** — effectively worthless
- **The ruble itself** — fell from ~1:1 vs USD (1990) to ~100:1 by December 1991
- **State enterprise shares** — many became worthless as enterprises closed
- **Supply chains** — trade collapsed; the economy reverted to barter in many sectors

**What Held Value:**
- **Hard currency (USD, Deutschmark, Swiss Francs)** — immediate and massive flight from ruble. Those with access to foreign currency preserved and often multiplied wealth
- **Privatized apartments** — the July 1991 privatization law allowed residents to claim ownership of state-assigned apartments. This was one of the best practical moves; the majority of Russians still live in privatized Soviet apartments
- **Dachas (country houses)** — rural properties with agricultural potential held value better than urban real estate
- **Natural resource extraction assets** — companies controlling oil, gas, and minerals could generate foreign currency. The oligarchs who acquired these assets during privatization became billionaires
- **Physical goods and barter commodities** — bricks were literally a better store of value than rubles; goods-for-debt replaced monetary transactions
- **Physical gold** — maintained value (though the Soviet state was forced to dump 135 tons to pay for food imports)

**Best Practical Investment:**
1. Convert ruble savings to hard currency as early as possible (late 1990)
2. Privatize your apartment immediately when the law passed (July 1991)
3. Acquire natural resource assets during the privatization fire sales
4. Move capital offshore (Cyprus, Switzerland, London property)

**Key Insight:** The Soviet collapse was uniquely devastating for ruble holders because the speed of the currency collapse (2,300% in one year) gave people almost no time to react. The lesson is that in regime-change scenarios, the window to convert domestic currency to hard assets is extremely narrow. Those who moved first (or who already had foreign currency) were the winners. Those who trusted the system and waited were destroyed.

## SUPPLEMENTARY CASE STUDIES

---

### 5. Weimar Hyperinflation (1921-23)

**Crisis Type:** Currency Collapse (Type 3) — extreme version of inflation

**Key Data:**
- Prices doubled every ~3.7 days at peak
- The Reichsmark lost 99.9%+ of its purchasing power
- A loaf of bread that cost 250 marks in January 1923 cost 200 billion marks by November

**Winners:** Gold holders (THE asset that preserved wealth), foreign currency holders (especially USD and Swiss Francs), real asset owners (land, factories), anyone who borrowed in marks and repaid in worthless currency

**Losers:** Cash holders, bondholders, anyone on fixed income, savers, pensioners

**Unique feature:** People with mortgages denominated in marks effectively had their debts wiped out — one of the rare cases where *debtors* benefited from a crisis.

---

### 6. French Assignat Collapse (1789-1796)

**Crisis Type:** Currency Collapse (Type 3) triggered by revolution

**Key Data:**
- Assignats depreciated 18-20% by 1791, then to 50% of face by Feb 1792
- By April 1795: down to 8% of face value
- Backed by confiscated church lands, but overprinting destroyed confidence

**Winners:** Those who purchased confiscated national lands with small down payments in depreciating currency — got real assets for almost nothing. Farmers who demanded gold/silver (specie) for produce. Anyone holding physical precious metals.

**Losers:** Assignat holders, creditors paid in depreciating paper, urban workers paid in assignats.

**Unique feature:** Land originally confiscated from the Church became the most desired asset. The assignats were theoretically backed by this land, but overissuance destroyed the linkage.

### 7. Great Depression (1929-33)

**Crisis Type:** Deflationary Panic (Type 2)

**Key Data:**
- Stock market: Dow fell 89% from peak to trough (1929-1932)
- Manhattan real estate: -67%
- Agricultural land: -40%
- Crop prices: -60%
- Unemployment: peaked at 25%
- GDP: contracted ~30%

**Winners:** Cash holders (deflation increased purchasing power), gold holders (fixed price of $20.67/oz meant real value rose as everything else fell), long-term government bondholders, anyone with liquidity to buy distressed assets at the trough

**Losers:** Equity investors, leveraged speculators, farmers (land + crop price declines), bank depositors (bank failures wiped out savings pre-FDIC)

**Unique feature:** Roosevelt confiscated private gold holdings in 1933 (Executive Order 6102), then revalued gold from $20.67 to $35.00/oz — a 69% devaluation of the dollar. Gold holders were forcibly converted to cash at the old price, then saw gold revalued higher. This is the confiscation risk inherent in gold during state crises.

### 8. 1970s Stagflation (1971-1980)

**Crisis Type:** Inflationary/Stagflationary (Type 1)

**Key Data:**
- CPI inflation peaked at 14.8% (1980)
- Unemployment peaked at 9% (1975)
- Fed funds rate hit 20% (1981, Volcker shock)
- Misery Index (inflation + unemployment) tripled
- Real GDP growth stagnated

**Winners:**
- **Gold:** ~2,000% appreciation ($35 to $800/oz) — the definitive inflation hedge case study
- **Oil:** surged on OPEC embargo and Iranian revolution
- **Commodities broadly:** strong performance
- **Real estate:** appreciated nominally (but mortgage rates hit 18%, freezing transactions)
- **Farmland:** appreciated with commodity prices

**Losers:** Bondholders (deeply negative real returns), stock investors (S&P 500 returned ~0% nominal over the decade, negative real), cash holders (purchasing power eroded 50%+)

**Key insight:** This is gold's strongest historical case. If you're modeling an inflationary crisis, the 1970s is the primary reference period.

---

### 9. 2008 Global Financial Crisis

**Crisis Type:** Started as Bubble Pop (Type 4 — housing), evolved into Deflationary Panic (Type 2)

**Key Data:**
- S&P 500: -57% (Oct 2007 to Mar 2009)
- Gold: +25% during recession, +166% by 2011
- Farmland: +30% (2007-2009)
- Art (Mei Moses Index): -27% (2007-2009)
- Oil: -53% in 4 months (summer 2008)
- US Treasuries: positive returns (flight-to-safety, negative beta to equities)
- Greek bonds (later): 50% haircut in 2012 restructuring

**Winners:** US Treasury holders, gold holders (especially post-initial panic), farmland owners, anyone with cash to deploy at the March 2009 trough

**Losers:** Bank equity holders, mortgage-backed security holders, leveraged anything, oil longs (in the acute phase), peripheral European sovereign debt holders

**Key insight:** Gold initially fell during the September 2008 panic (forced liquidation across all assets) before recovering. This timing gap matters — if you need liquidity *during* the acute panic, gold may not provide it. It's a medium-term crisis hedge, not an intraday one.

---

### 10. COVID-19 Crash (2020)

**Crisis Type:** Deflationary Panic (Type 2) — but extremely brief due to massive fiscal/monetary response

**Key Data:**
- S&P 500: -34% in 23 trading days (fastest bear market entry ever)
- Recovery: full recovery within ~5 months
- Gold: +32% for 2020 ($1,575 to $2,072.50)
- US Treasuries: positive returns (safe haven)
- Farmland: low/negative returns (but income component stable)
- Oil: briefly went NEGATIVE ($-37/barrel, April 2020)

**Winners:** Anyone who held through the crash (full recovery), gold holders, Treasury holders, tech/remote-work equities

**Losers:** Those who panic-sold at the trough, oil longs (unprecedented negative pricing), travel/hospitality equities, anyone short volatility

**Key insight:** This crisis was unique in that the monetary/fiscal response was so massive and rapid that the "crisis" lasted only months. This makes it a poor reference for sustained crises. The main lesson is that not all crashes are sustained — sometimes the right move is simply not to sell.

## Key Patterns

1. **Gold is the only asset that performed well across the majority of crisis types** — but it failed or was confiscated in deflationary crises (Depression) and showed timing risk in acute panics (2008 initial sell-off)

2. **Productive land is the most durable store of value across civilizational-scale crises** — it's the foundation of wealth when institutions fail, but it's illiquid and failed in the 1981-82 farm crisis

3. **Government bonds are either the best or worst asset** — entirely dependent on the creditworthiness of the issuing state

4. **Cash is excellent in deflation and terrible in inflation** — the simplest variable to assess

5. **Equities almost always crash during crises** — even "defensive" sectors fall 30%+. The question is whether they recover (2020: yes; 1929: took 25 years)

6. **The most important decision is crisis-type classification**, not individual asset selection
