## 1. Nixon Closing Gold Window (1971) + 1973 OPEC Oil Embargo / Nifty Fifty Crash

### Gold Window Closing: August 15, 1971

- **Fixed gold price under Bretton Woods:** $35.00/oz since 1934 (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_shock)
- **Nixon's action:** On August 15, 1971, Nixon unilaterally suspended dollar-to-gold convertibility, ending the Bretton Woods system (Source: https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/gold-convertibility-ends)
- **Smithsonian Agreement (December 1971):** Dollar devalued — gold price raised to $38.00/oz (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_shock)
- **Second devaluation (February 14, 1973):** Official rate raised to $42.22/oz, then dollar left to float entirely (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_standard)
- **October 1976:** US government officially removed references to gold from statutes; dollar fully fiat (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_standard)

The trajectory was: $35 fixed → $38 (Dec 1971) → $42.22 official (Feb 1973) → freely floating, rising from ~$100 in 1973 to ~$200 in 1974, surging to ~$590 annual average by 1980, with an intraday peak of $850 in January 1980. *These intermediate values are approximate, not individually source-verified.*

### Nifty Fifty Stocks

**Definition:** "One-decision" blue-chip growth stocks of the late 1960s–early 1970s, commonly trading at P/E ratios of 50x or higher (vs. long-term market average of 15–20x). (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)

**Company list (partial — frequently cited members):** Avon Products, Polaroid, McDonald's, Xerox, IBM, Eastman Kodak, Johnson & Johnson, Coca-Cola, Disney, Walmart, Pfizer, Merck, 3M, General Electric, Texas Instruments, Eli Lilly, Procter & Gamble, PepsiCo (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)

**P/E ratios:** Professor Jeremy Siegel (Stocks for the Long Run) analyzed the Nifty Fifty and found that companies routinely sold at P/E ratios above 50, with "trading at fifty times earnings or higher ... common" (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)

**Specific peak P/E ratios (Siegel's data, 1972 peak):**
- NOT FOUND in verifiable static sources. Siegel's book (Stocks for the Long Run, 4th ed.) is the canonical source for individual P/E tables (Avon Products ~61x, Polaroid ~91x, McDonald's ~83x, Xerox ~47x, etc.) but these cannot be source-verified via URL without a subscription. Flag: widely cited but book-only.

**Performance:** "The long bear market of the 1970s which began with the 1973–74 stock market crash and lasted until 1982 caused valuations of the nifty fifty to fall to low levels along with the rest of the market, with most of these stocks under-performing the broader market averages." (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)

**Best performer on the list:** Walmart — 29.65% compounded annualized return over 29 years from its 1972 NYSE listing (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)

### OPEC Oil Prices: Pre- and Post-Embargo

- **Pre-embargo (1960s):** Oil price remained at $1.80/barrel throughout the 1960s (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)
- **Pre-embargo 1971:** $2.00/barrel (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)
- **OPEC summit price hike (October 16, 1973):** Oil price raised from $3.01 to $5.12 per barrel at Kuwait City summit (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)
- **Post-embargo peak (by March 1974):** Oil rose to nearly $12/barrel globally — an increase of "nearly 300%" from pre-embargo levels of ~$3 (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)
- **Embargo period:** October 1973 – March 1974 (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)

### S&P 500 Recovery After Volcker-Era Bear Market

- **DJIA bottom:** August 1982 at 776 (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))
- **DJIA peak (pre-Black Monday):** August 25, 1987 at 2,722 — a gain of 250%+ over 5 years (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))
- **S&P 500 1982 full-year return:** +20.42% (Damodaran) — the bear market trough was mid-year 1982; the 1982 full-year positive return confirms recovery was well underway by year-end
- **S&P 500 1983 return:** +22.34% (Damodaran) — new highs achieved within roughly 1 year of the August 1982 trough
- **Interpretation:** The Volcker shock bear market (1981: -4.70%) bottomed in August 1982; the S&P 500 recovered prior peak levels within approximately 12–18 months of the trough, supported by Volcker pivoting to ease rates in mid-1982 (Fed Funds fell from 19% in 1981 to 8.95% year-end 1982)

### Gold Price During Volcker Era

- **January 21, 1980:** Gold hit intraday London Fix of $850/oz — the record high (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_fixing)
- **1980 annual average:** ~$590/oz (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_as_an_investment, 5-year table)
- **1985 annual average:** ~$327/oz (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_as_an_investment) — gold fell ~45% from its 1980 annual average as Volcker's policies tamed inflation

## 3. Black Monday (October 1987)

### Key Numbers for October 19, 1987

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987)

- **DJIA opened:** 2,246.74
- **DJIA closed:** 1,738.74
- **DJIA points lost:** 508 points
- **DJIA % decline:** 22.6% — "the largest one-day percentage drop in the history of the DJIA" (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))
- **S&P 500 decline on October 19:** The Wikipedia article confirms the crash hit the S&P 500 (95 S&P 500 stocks "opened late"), consistent with approximately 20% on that day (exact % for S&P is not separately stated in this source)
- **Worldwide losses:** Estimated at US$1.71 trillion (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))

### Pre-Crash Bull Market Context

- **DJIA August 1982 bottom:** 776 (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))
- **DJIA August 1987 peak:** 2,722 — a gain of 251% over 5 years (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))
- **DJIA year-end 1987:** 1,938.83 — full-year return of +2.26% (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average annual table)
- **S&P 500 full-year 1987 return:** +5.81% (Source: Damodaran, https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/New_Home_Page/datafile/histretSP.html) — the crash was severe intraday/intramonth but the year started high enough that the full-year return was still positive

### Pre-Crash Lead-Up (October 1987)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987)

- October 14: DJIA fell 95.46 points (3.81%) to 2,412.70
- October 15: DJIA fell another 57.61 points (2.39%)
- October 16: DJIA fell 108.35 points (4.6%)
- October 19: DJIA fell 508 points (22.6%) — the crash day

The selloff was part of a broader decline from the August 25 peak, not just a single-day event.

### 10-Year Treasury Yield Around Black Monday (Flight to Safety)

Source: FRED DGS10, https://fred.stlouisfed.org/graph/fredgraph.csv?id=DGS10

| Date | 10Y Yield |
|------|-----------|
| Oct 15, 1987 | 10.18% |

**Flight to safety confirmed:** 10-year Treasury yields dropped 143 basis points in just 7 trading days after Black Monday (from 10.23% on Oct 16 to 8.80% on Oct 26), reflecting a massive flight from equities into government bonds.

### Recovery Time

- **DJIA:** "Dow Jones begins to recover in November 1987" (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987) infobox)
- **Full return to pre-crash levels (US):** The Wikipedia article states "New York ... needing more than a year to achieve the same level of recovery" from the crash, while Japan returned to pre-crash levels within five months (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))
- **UK (FTSE 100):** Lost 23% in two days, stocks "did not begin to recover until 1989" — took approximately 2 years (Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987))

### International Market Declines (October 1987)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987)

- "All of the twenty-three major world markets experienced a sharp decline in October 1987"
- Eight markets declined 20–29%, three by 30–39% (Malaysia, Mexico, New Zealand), three by more than 40% (Hong Kong, Australia, Singapore)
- Worst: Hong Kong — 45.8% decline
- Least affected: Austria — 11.4% decline

### Bond and Gold Behavior

- **Bonds:** Flight to Treasuries clearly documented in FRED data above; 143bp yield drop in 7 days after crash
- **Gold behavior:** NOT FOUND with specific price numbers for October 19, 1987 from verifiable static sources accessed. Gold was in a general bear market from 1980–1999.

## 5. Soviet Union Collapse (1991–1993)

### Ruble Exchange Rate vs. USD

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_ruble

- **July 1992:** US$1 = 125 RUR (Russian ruble, post-Soviet currency)
- **Post-redenomination (1997):** New ruble (RUB) = 1,000 old rubles (RUR)
- By the mid-1990s: US$1 ≈ 6,000 RUR (before the 1998 redenomination)
- Trajectory: From ~125 RUR/USD (July 1992) to ~6,000 RUR/USD (before 1997 redenomination) — a 48x devaluation in roughly 5 years

**Pre-1992 Soviet ruble:** The Soviet ruble (SUR, ISO code) was replaced by the Russian ruble (RUR) at par in 1992 following the USSR's dissolution. The official Soviet exchange rate was highly artificial (~0.60 SUR/USD for tourists; black market rates much higher).

### Russian/Soviet CPI Inflation

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Russia

### Russia Voucher Privatization (1992)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatization_in_Russia

- **Program dates:** 1992–1994
- **Vouchers issued:** 144 million people received privatization vouchers
- **Privatized firms:** Ownership of 15,000 firms transferred via the voucher program
- **Participation:** "roughly 98 percent of the population participated"
- **Outcome:** "Most vouchers—and, hence, most shares—wound up being acquired by the management of the enterprises" or sold for cash by citizens who were "not well-informed about the nature of the program or were very poor"
- **Historical assessment:** Facilitated wealth transfer to a small group of oligarchs; described as economic catastrophe for ordinary Russians
