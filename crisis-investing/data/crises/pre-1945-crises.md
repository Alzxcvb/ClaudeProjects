## 1. Great Depression (1929–1933)

### Dow Jones Industrial Average

- **Peak:** September 3, 1929, closing price **381.17** (Source: [Federal Reserve History / FRED confirmed via Federal Reserve History essay on Stock Market Crash of 1929](https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/stock-market-crash-of-1929); independently confirmed by [Wikipedia – Wall Street Crash of 1929](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wall_Street_crash_of_1929) and [Slickcharts 1929 Crash Chart](https://www.slickcharts.com/dowjones/crash/1929))
- **Trough:** July 8, 1932, closing price **41.22** (Source: same references above)
- **Peak-to-trough decline:** approximately **89%** (Source: same; characterized as "its lowest value of the twentieth century, 89 percent below its peak")
- **Recovery to 1929 high:** Not until **November 23, 1954** — approximately 25 years (Source: [Wikipedia – Closing milestones of the Dow Jones Industrial Average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closing_milestones_of_the_Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average))

### Gold Price: EO 6102 and Revaluation

- **Pre-1933 gold price (fixed):** **$20.67 per troy ounce** — the statutory gold content of the US dollar since the Coinage Act of 1792 (Source: [Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_Order_6102))
- **Executive Order 6102 signed:** **April 5, 1933**, by President Franklin D. Roosevelt, requiring citizens to surrender all gold coin, gold bullion, and gold certificates in excess of $100 face value to Federal Reserve Banks (Source: [Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_Order_6102); primary text at [American Presidency Project](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/executive-order-6102-forbidding-the-hoarding-gold-coin-gold-bullion-and-gold-certificates))
- **Surrender deadline:** **May 1, 1933** (Source: Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102)
- **Personal exemption:** Up to **$100 in gold coins** (~5 troy ounces) could be retained (Source: Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102)
- **Penalty for violation:** Fine up to **$10,000** and/or imprisonment up to **10 years** (Source: Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102)
- **Gold Reserve Act of 1934 signed:** **January 30, 1934** — Congress authorized the President to devalue the gold dollar (Source: [EBSCO Research Starters — Gold Reserve Act of 1934](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/law/gold-reserve-act-1934))
- **Revaluation to $35.00 per troy ounce:** Effective with the Gold Reserve Act of 1934 — government profit on surrendered gold: citizens received $20.67/oz, government revalued to $35.00/oz, representing a **69.3% devaluation of the dollar** (Source: Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102; [Federal Reserve History — Roosevelt's Gold Program](https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/roosevelts-gold-program))
- **Gold restrictions repealed:** December 31, 1974, under President Gerald Ford (Source: Wikipedia — Executive Order 6102)

### US Real Estate Prices

- **National nominal decline 1929–1932:** approximately **-67%** from peak (Q3 1929 high to end-1932) (Source: [Harvard Business School — Real Estate Prices During the Roaring Twenties and the Great Depression](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=41283), Nicholas & Scherbina 2013, *Real Estate Economics*, as reported by [EconLib](https://www.econlib.org/archives/2011/04/home_prices_dur.html))
- **Robert Shiller inflation-adjusted index:** Fell from approximately **74 to 69** between 1929 and 1933 — roughly **-7% in real terms**. Historical average 1890–1916 was 99.5; the entire 1917–1944 period averaged 74.2, an historical anomaly. (Source: [EconLib — Home Prices During the Great Depression](https://www.econlib.org/archives/2011/04/home_prices_dur.html), citing Shiller data)
- **Residential construction:** Fell **95%** between 1929 and 1933 (Source: [EconLib / Encyclopedia.com — Housing 1929–1941](https://www.encyclopedia.com/education/news-and-education-magazines/housing-1929-1941))
- **Agricultural land:** Declined approximately **-40%** in nominal value 1929–1932, broadly consistent with the -67% urban real estate figure but mitigated by land's productive capacity (Source: [key-numbers.md](../key-numbers.md) — existing research compilation; original sourcing is USDA historical series)
- **Manhattan real estate:** A typical property bought in 1920 retained only **56%** of its initial nominal value two decades later (1940). High-end properties "strongly co-moved with the stock market between 1929 and 1932." (Source: Harvard Business School / Nicholas & Scherbina study cited at [EconLib](https://www.econlib.org/archives/2011/04/home_prices_dur.html))

### Farm Commodity Prices

- **Wheat (Chicago):** Fell from **$1.40 per bushel** (July 1929) to **$0.49 per bushel** — a decline of approximately **65%** in two years. Recovered above $1.00 by mid-1934. (Source: [EconomicsHelp.org / Wikipedia — Great Depression](https://www.economicshelp.org/blog/162985/economics/unemployment-during-the-great-depression/); search-confirmed figure from multiple sources)
- **Corn (Minnesota, 1932):** **$0.28 per bushel** at trough (Source: [CUNY OpenEd — US History](https://opened.cuny.edu/courseware/lesson/439/student-old/?task=4))
- **Farm income:** Between 1929 and 1930, real (CPI-deflated) nonfarm income fell 6% while **real farm income fell 25%** (Source: [CEPR VoxEU — Farm Product Prices, Redistribution, and the Early US Great Depression](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/farm-product-prices-redistribution-and-early-great-depression-us))

## 2. Panic of 1907

### Stock Market Decline

- **Dow Jones at January 1906 high:** **103** (Source: [Wikipedia — Panic of 1907](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1907))
- **By July 1906:** -18% from January peak (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **September 1906 to March 1907:** Lost **7.7%** of market capitalization (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **March 9–26, 1907:** Additional **-9.8%** decline (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **First nine months of 1907:** Stocks down **-24.4%** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Dow Jones trough:** **53 on November 15, 1907** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Peak-to-trough decline:** Approximately **50%** from the 1906 peak, ranking as the **8th-largest decline in US stock market history** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)

### Economic Contraction

- **NBER recession dates:** May 1907 to June 1908 (Source: [NY Fed Liberty Street Economics — The Final Crisis Chronicle: The Panic of 1907](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2016/11/the-final-crisis-chronicle-the-panic-of-1907-and-the-birth-of-the-fed/))
- **Real GDP contraction:** More than **10%** — roughly double the 2007–08 recession (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics above)
- **Industrial production:** Dropped **11%** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Imports:** Fell **26%** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Unemployment:** Rose to **8%** from under 3% (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Bankruptcies:** "Second-highest volume of bankruptcies to that date" (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)

### Trigger Events and Timeline

- **October 14, 1907:** United Copper Company stock rose from **$39 to $52** per share (F. Augustus Heinze's attempted corner) (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **October 15:** Stock reached nearly **$60** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **October 16:** Stock collapsed to **$30**, then to **$10** by day's end — corner failed (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **October 22:** Knickerbocker Trust (second-largest trust in New York) withdrew **$8 million in cash** in under three hours before suspending operations (Source: [EH.net Encyclopedia — The Panic of 1907](https://eh.net/encyclopedia/the-panic-of-1907/))

### Trust Company Bank Runs

- **Trust Company of America** withdrawals during the run:
  - October 22: approximately **$1.5 million**
  - October 23: **$13 million** in a single day (from total deposits of nearly $60 million)
  - October 24: **$8–9 million**
  - Total paid out over two weeks: **$47.5 million**
(Source: EH.net Encyclopedia above and NY Fed Liberty Street Economics above)

### J.P. Morgan and Government Interventions

- **October 23:** Morgan channeled approximately **$8.25 million** from trust companies to Trust Company of America to prevent collapse (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **October 24:** Morgan organized 14 bank presidents to pledge **$23.6 million** within 10 minutes; **$19 million had been loaned out** before market close (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **October 24:** Treasury Secretary Cortelyou deposited approximately **$25 million** of Treasury funds (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia; NY Fed Liberty Street above)
- **Total Treasury deposits October 21–31:** **$37.6 million** plus an additional **$36 million in small bills** provided for bank runs (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia)
- **October 25:** Morgan secured **$9.7 million** to stabilize the exchange (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **October 29:** Morgan purchased **$30 million** in New York City bonds to prevent city bankruptcy (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Rockefeller deposit:** **$10 million** in National City Bank (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Morgan's personal losses:** Approximately **$21 million** (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)

### Call Money and Currency

- **Peak call money rate, October 24:** **100%** (no takers at this level); rate remained at approximately **50%** through October 25 (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia above)
- **New York Clearing House loan certificates total:** **$100 million** issued (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)
- **Clearinghouse certificates in first three weeks:** Over **$110 million** (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia)
- **National circulation of currency substitutes:** Nearly **$500 million** (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia)

### Gold Flows

- **San Francisco earthquake (April 1906)** triggered large gold outflows from London to US: earthquake-related payments represented **40% of seasonally adjusted British gold exports for all of 1906**, nearly **2.5x** more than British gold flows to any other country in 1906 (Source: [NY Fed Liberty Street Economics — Panic of 1907](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2016/11/the-final-crisis-chronicle-the-panic-of-1907-and-the-birth-of-the-fed/))
- **Bank of England response:** Raised rates by **250 basis points to 6.0%** in September–October 1906, hitting a 16-year low in reserves-to-deposits ratio, and effectively banned acceptance of American "finance bills" (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics above)
- **Recovery mechanism:** Restriction of deposit convertibility created a **cash premium**, causing a substantial influx of gold imports that "appear to have been instrumental in spurring recovery" (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics above)

### Recovery Timeline

- **October 1907–January 1908:** Main crisis period (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia)
- **January 1908:** Convertibility "widely restored" (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia)
- **March 1908:** Some currency substitutes still circulating (Source: EH.net Encyclopedia)
- **May 1908:** Congress passed Aldrich-Vreeland Act, established National Monetary Commission (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **December 1913:** Federal Reserve Act signed into law (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **1914:** Twelve Federal Reserve Banks opened (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)

### J.P. Morgan's Structural Power (Context)

The Pujo Committee later found Morgan & Co. officers sat on boards of **112 corporations** with combined market capitalization of **$22.5 billion**, out of total NYSE capitalization of approximately **$26.5 billion** — explaining why Morgan's personal intervention could stabilize the system in the absence of a central bank. (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1907)

**Peak-to-trough decline:** From £1,000 (early August 1720) to approximately £100–124 (December 1720) = roughly **-88% to -90%** over approximately 5 months. (Source: Historic UK gives £124 as year-end trough; Wikipedia gives "£100 per share before year's end")

**Note on the July price:** A price of ~£950 is sometimes cited for July 1720, but this reflects a **forward contract price** while the transfer books were closed — it is considered less reliable than the August cash price of £1,000. (Source: Dave Smant site above, noting "less reliable forward contract price since transfer books were closed")

### Total Shares Outstanding

At the September 1720 level: **212,012 shares** outstanding; at £775/share = implied market cap of approximately **£164 million** (Source: Dave Smant — South Sea Bubble 1720)

**August 1720 dividend announcement:** 30% for the current year; 50% for future years — announcements that proved unsustainable and accelerated the collapse. (Source: Dave Smant — South Sea Bubble 1720)

### Bank of England and East India Company Comparison

- **Bank of England shares:** Rose approximately **+60%** from January 1 to June 22, 1720, then fell back (Source: [Yale SOM — Looking Back 300 Years at the South Sea Bubble](https://som.yale.edu/blog/looking-back-300-years-at-the-south-sea-bubble-of-1720); citing Frehen, Goetzmann, Rouwenhorst research database)
- **East India Company shares:** Rose approximately **+70%** over the same January–June 1720 period, then fell back (Source: Yale SOM above, citing same dataset; also [East India Company Shareholders and the South Sea Bubble, University of St Andrews](https://www.st-andrews.ac.uk/~wwwecon/CDMA/papers/wp1109.pdf))
- **Contrast:** Both the Bank of England and East India Company saw modest gains relative to South Sea, with "no boom and investor optimism of similar magnitude" — speculation was concentrated in South Sea Company (Source: Yale SOM / Frehen, Goetzmann, Rouwenhorst)

### UK Government Debt Structure (1720)

Total government debt of approximately **£50 million** structured as follows:
- Bank of England held: **£3.4 million**
- East India Company held: **£3.2 million**
- South Sea Company held: **£11.7 million**
- Privately held redeemable debt: **£16.5 million**
- Irredeemable annuities: **£15 million**

(Source: Wikipedia — South Sea Company)

The South Sea Company ultimately acquired **85%** of the redeemable debt and **80%** of the irredeemable annuities through the 1720 conversion. Government agreed to pay **5% annually** to South Sea until 1727, then **4% thereafter**. (Source: Dave Smant — South Sea Bubble 1720)

### Government Debt Yield/Interest Rate Context

**Rates before the South Sea scheme (1717–1719):** The Bank of England and the South Sea Company had already agreed to reduce government annuities to **5 percent** in 1717 (Source: search-confirmed, multiple sources including Wikipedia — South Sea Company)

**Structure of British government debt in 1719:** The £50 million national debt comprised mixed instruments — some paying **14 percent** (lottery annuities), **9 percent** (annuities to 1742), and **7 percent** (annuities maturing as late as 1807). Short annuities had ~22 years remaining; long annuities had 72–87 years remaining. (Source: [Wikipedia — South Sea Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Sea_Company))

**South Sea scheme terms (1720):** The Company agreed to pay the government an unconditional **£4 million** plus a conditional sum equal to 4.5 years' purchase on all irredeemable debts converted by March 1722. The government's interest cost on converted debt would be: **5% annually until midsummer 1727**, then **4% thereafter on all debt held by the Company** — a substantial reduction from the pre-existing rates of 7–14% on the irregular annuity instruments. (Source: [Wikipedia — South Sea Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Sea_Company); [Dave Smant — South Sea Bubble 1720](https://sites.google.com/site/davesmant/monetary-economics/famous-first-bubbles/south-sea-bubble))

**Government Consolidated Funds (Consols) — sourcing note:** True Consols (consolidated 3% perpetual annuities) were not created until **1751**, when Chancellor Henry Pelham consolidated earlier government annuities. Yield data for Consols therefore begins in the 1750s — the earliest available series (Napoleon Series, citing historical records) starts at 1790: yield **3.9%** in 1790. Pre-1750 British government borrowing costs must be inferred from the specific annuity rates described above. No continuous pre-1750 government yield series was retrievable from live sources. The closest approximation: the South Sea scheme was designed to reduce UK government borrowing costs from a weighted average of 7–14% toward 4–5%. (Sources: [Napoleon Series — British 3% Consols Annual Average Price and Yield](https://www.napoleon-series.org/research/abstract/economics/finance/c_consols.html); Wikipedia — South Sea Company)

### Pamphleteer Estimate of Fair Value

Archibald Hutcheson, a contemporary critic, estimated the stock's actual fundamental value at approximately **£200 per share** — the shares peaked at 5x this intrinsic value estimate. (Source: Historic UK — South Sea Bubble)

### Individual Losses

- **Isaac Newton:** Lost approximately **£20,000** (Wikipedia) or "approximately £40 million in today's currency" (Historic UK). Newton reportedly said, "I can calculate the motion of heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people." (Sources: Wikipedia — South Sea Company; Historic UK — South Sea Bubble)

### Legislative Response

- **Bubble Act of 1720:** Passed same year, restricted creation of joint-stock companies without royal charter (Source: Historic UK — South Sea Bubble)
- **South Sea Company continued trading** until **1853** after restructuring (Source: Historic UK — South Sea Bubble)

### Data Note

Day-by-day interpolated stock prices for South Sea, Bank of England, and East India Company are available in the academic database of Frehen, Goetzmann, and Rouwenhorst, "New Evidence on the First Financial Bubble," *Journal of Financial Economics*, 2013. Dataset downloadable at [Yale SOM — South Sea Bubble 1720 Project](https://som.yale.edu/centers/international-center-for-finance/data/historical-financial-research-data/south-seas-bubble-1720). The figures above are drawn from secondary sources citing that dataset or contemporaneous records.

**Gain from par to peak:** From 500 to 10,000 livres = **+1,900%** in approximately 18 months (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics above)

### Share Issuances

- **Initial issuance (1719):** **50,000 shares** at 500 livres (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **Second issuance:** **300,000 shares** at 500 livres (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **Total by mid-1719:** Over **600,000 shares** issued (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **Total by 1719 (Britannica):** approximately **625,000 shares** (Source: Britannica — Mississippi Bubble)
- **Payment terms (second issuance):** 50 livres monthly installments over 10 months (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)

### French Government Debt Before Law's System

John Law inherited a fiscal catastrophe (1715, death of Louis XIV):
- **Royal debts:** approximately **3 billion livres** (Source: Mises Institute above)
- **Annual income:** **145 million livres** (Source: Mises Institute)
- **Annual expenditure:** **142 million livres** (Source: Mises Institute)
- **Interest obligations:** **220 million livres** per year, with only **3 million** available (Source: Mises Institute)
- **Existing debt traded at:** approximately **80% discount** to face value on secondary market (Source: Mises Institute)
- **National debt total (as Law framed it):** **1.5 billion livres** — the sum Law proposed the Mississippi Company would repay (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)

Law purchased the right to collect all French taxes for **52 million livres**, centralizing revenue collection under the Mississippi Company/Banque Royale system. (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)

### Banque Royale Money Supply Expansion

- **Banque Générale (private, 1716):** Initially issued **60 million livres** in notes backed by specie (Source: Mises Institute)
- **Authorization upon nationalization (1718):** Up to **1 billion livres** additional notes authorized for the renamed Banque Royale (Source: Mises Institute)
- **By end of 1719:** Approximately **1 billion livres** in new inflationary notes had been created (Source: Mises Institute; confirmed by NY Fed article: "amount of paper money in circulation increased by around a billion livres over the course of 1719")
- **By May 1720 (peak):** Total banknotes in circulation reached **2 billion livres** — a **50-fold increase** from the original Banque Générale issuance, and "roughly double the quantity of gold and silver coins in circulation" (Source: [Mises Institute](https://mises.org/mises-wire/john-law-and-mississippi-bubble-300-years-later))

### French Livre Exchange Rate vs Sterling

The livre's exchange rate against English sterling:

- **Normal/pre-bubble rate:** approximately **9 old pence** (per livre) (Source: Mises Institute; confirmed by multiple search results)
- **September 1720:** Collapsed to **2½ old pence** per livre — a decline of approximately **72%** (Source: Mises Institute; [Dave Smant — Mississippi Bubble 1720](https://sites.google.com/site/davesmant/monetary-economics/famous-first-bubbles/mississippi-bubble))
- **Final months of 1720:** **No sterling price quoted** for paper livres — they had become effectively worthless, with "the public having lost all confidence in them" (Source: Mises Institute)
- **Entire inflation episode duration:** **4.5 years** (May 5, 1716–November 1720) (Source: Mises Institute)

### Inflation in France

- **Food prices:** Rose approximately **60%** by mid-1720 (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **General product prices:** Initially fell approximately **one-third** due to Law's tax efficiency reforms, then reversed and soared (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **Paris real estate:** Apartment rents on the Rue de Quincampoix (the street where shares were traded) increased **16-fold** at peak of the bubble (Source: Winton — The Mississippi Bubble)

### Outcome for French Shareholders

- By September 1721, shares had returned to par (**500 livres**) — a **-95% decline** from the ~10,000 livre peak (Source: Wikipedia — John Law's Company)
- Law was dismissed by the Regent Philippe d'Orléans at the end of 1720 and fled France in December 1720 (Source: Britannica — Mississippi Bubble)
- The French state absorbed the Mississippi Company's debts and the Banque Royale's obligations by raising taxes (Source: Britannica — Mississippi Bubble)
- France's experience poisoned attitudes toward paper money and joint-stock companies for nearly a century, a significant factor in French financial underdevelopment through the 18th century

## 5. Long Depression (1873–1879)

### Trigger: Jay Cooke & Co. Failure

- **Date of failure:** September 18, 1873 — Jay Cooke & Company, America's premier banking house, suspended deposit withdrawals (Source: [NY Fed Liberty Street Economics — Long Depression and Panic of 1873](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2016/02/crisis-chronicles-the-long-depression-and-the-panic-of-1873/))
- **Cause:** Northern Pacific Railway bond price depression due to cost overruns and post-Crédit Mobilier distrust; Cooke had been the lead underwriter for Northern Pacific (Source: multiple sources including [Smithsonian Magazine](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/history/robber-baron-gamble-railroads-brought-down-bank-plunged-us-into-first-great-depression-panic-1873-180982877/))
- **Railroad debt buildup:** Between 1868 and 1873, **33,000 miles** (53,000 km) of new railroad track was laid; total railroad bond debt grew from **$416 million in 1867 to $2.23 billion by 1874** — a **436% increase** (Source: [Wikipedia — Panic of 1873](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1873))

### Stock Market and NYSE

- **NYSE closure:** Closed for **ten days** starting September 20, 1873 — one of the longest closures in exchange history (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1873; [Wikipedia — Long Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_Depression))
- **New York City bank reserves:** Declined from **$50 million to $17 million** between September and October 1873 (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1873)
- **Specific stock market decline percentage — Not retrieved from live source.** The NYSE fell significantly in 1873 — described as having "plummeted" — but a single authoritative peak-to-trough percentage for 1873–1876 was not available in the sources accessed. Two primary data series contain these values but were inaccessible:
  - **Cowles Commission All-Common-Stock Index:** Monthly data January 1871–December 1956, available via [FRED — M1125AUSM343NNBR](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/M1125AUSM343NNBR) (returned HTTP 403 during research)
  - **NBER/Macaulay American Railroad Stock Prices:** Monthly data January 1857–December 1929, available via [FRED — M1105AUSM505NNBR](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/M1105AUSM505NNBR) (also returned HTTP 403)
  - Researchers needing the specific percentage should query the FRED series directly or consult F.R. Macaulay, *Some Theoretical Problems Suggested By The Movements of Interest Rates, Bond Yields, And Stock Prices in the United States Since 1856* (NBER, 1938)

### Railroad Failures and Defaults

- **By November 1873:** Approximately **55 railroads** had failed (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1873)
- **By first anniversary (September 1874):** Another **60 railroads** had gone bankrupt — total approximately 115 (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1873)
- **Total railroad failures:** **89 railroads** failed over the full depression (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **Total business failures:** **18,000 businesses** went bankrupt 1873–1875 (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression and Panic of 1873)
- **Total debt in default:** Over **$1 billion** (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **Railroad construction collapse:** From **7,500 miles** built in 1872 to only **1,600 miles** in 1875 (Source: Wikipedia — Panic of 1873)

### Unemployment and Economic Impact

- **Peak unemployment nationally:** **8.25%** in 1878 (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **New York City unemployment:** Up to **25% of workers** unemployed during winter 1873–1874 (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression and NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **National unemployed count:** Approximately **1 million workers** (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **Nominal wages:** Declined by **one-quarter** nationally during the 1870s; as much as **one-half** in some regions like Pennsylvania (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **Manufacturing output declines (1872–1876):**
  - Overall manufacturing: **-10%**
  - Durable goods: **-30%**
  - Iron and steel: **-45%**
  - Construction: **-30%**
  (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)

### Duration

- **US depression:** October 1873 to March 1879 = **65 months** — the longest contraction ever identified by NBER (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **International:** Continued until 1879 in France and Britain; Britain experienced two decades of stagnation (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)

### Agricultural Commodity Prices

- **Wheat and corn average prices 1870–1873:** Corn averaged **$0.463/bushel**, wheat averaged **$1.174/bushel** (Source: [Wikipedia — Long Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_Depression))
- **By 1890–1893 (20 years later):** Corn had fallen to **$0.412/bushel** (-11%), wheat to **$0.707/bushel** (-40%) (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **Cotton:** Fell nearly **50%** in just five years from 1872 to 1877 (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- **Grain prices in 1894:** Only **one-third** of their 1867 levels — a 67% decline over 27 years (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)

**Note on near-term (1873–1875) specific prices:** Precise annual wheat and corn price data for 1873–1875 was not available in the sources retrieved. The figures above cover the broader trend across the Long Depression decade. The US Bureau of the Census, *Historical Statistics of the United States, Colonial Times to 1957* (Washington, D.C., 1960), pp. 122–123 contains original commodity price series for this period.

### Gold and the Bimetallic Controversy

- **Coinage Act of 1873:** Demonetized silver (dropped silver dollar from list of coins), reducing domestic money supply and raising interest rates — this act was later condemned as the "Crime of '73" by debtors and farmers who felt it caused deflation (Source: [Wikipedia — Panic of 1873](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1873))
- **Money supply relief attempt (March 1874):** Congress passed bill to add **$400 million in Greenbacks** and **$100 million** to money supply — vetoed by President Grant (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **Resumption Act (January 1875):** Committed US government to reinstating the gold standard in four years (Source: NY Fed Liberty Street Economics)
- **Gold returned to gold standard:** January 1, 1879 (de facto resumption of specie payments) (Source: general historical record)

**Note on gold price during this period:** Under the gold standard, the official gold price was fixed. The "price" controversy was about silver — specifically whether to re-monetize silver at a 16:1 ratio to gold. Specific gold coin premia or discount data for the 1873–1879 period was not retrieved from live sources.

### Industrial Production Context (Recovery)

Despite the depression, US industrial capacity continued expanding:
- Iron production grew from **11 million to 23 million tons** between 1870–1890 (doubled) (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)
- Steel production grew from **500,000 to 11 million tons** (twentyfold increase) (Source: Wikipedia — Long Depression)

This illustrates the paradox of the Long Depression: nominal prices and wages fell while real output expanded, reflecting productivity gains and railroad-enabled market integration.
