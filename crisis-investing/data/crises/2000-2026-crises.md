## 1. Asian Financial Crisis (1997–1998) + LTCM Collapse

### Thai Baht Devaluation
- **Date of devaluation:** July 2, 1997 (float announced, peg abandoned)
- **Pre-crisis peg:** 25 baht per USD
- **Post-float low:** 56 baht per USD (January 1998)
- **Peak decline ~55% vs USD** (from 25 to 56 per USD)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1997_Asian_financial_crisis

### Indonesian Rupiah
- **Pre-crisis rate:** ~2,600 rupiah per USD (June 1997)
- **Crisis low:** 16,800 rupiah per USD (June 1998); also above 14,000 during January and June–July 1998
- **Peak decline: ~83–85% vs USD** (2,600 → 16,800)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1997_Asian_financial_crisis

### Malaysian Ringgit
- Pre-crisis: above 2.50 to USD
- Crisis low: under 4.57 to USD (January 1998) — **~50% decline**
- PM Mahathir then **fixed the ringgit at 3.80 to the USD** and imposed strict capital controls (September 1, 1998)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1997_Asian_financial_crisis

### South Korean Won / KOSPI
- Won weakened from ~800 to more than 1,700 per USD — **~53% decline vs USD**
- KOSPI: From peak of 980.9 (end of April 1996) to low of 31.96 (June 16, 1998) — **~97% peak-to-trough decline over 1996–1998**
- Note: The 97% figure covers the full peak-to-trough; the crisis-year 1997 decline alone was severe but the absolute low came in 1998.
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KOSPI and https://www.dallasfed.org/~/media/documents/research/efr/2001/efr0104c.pdf

### US S&P 500 — Asian Crisis Period (1997–1998)
- S&P 500 **1997 annual return: +33.10%** — US market was largely unaffected in 1997
- S&P 500 **1998 annual return: +28.34%** — also up for the full year despite mid-year panic
- However: **S&P 500 corrected ~19% peak-to-trough in 1998** (July 17 peak to October 8 trough) due to Russia/LTCM contagion, before recovering sharply
- Source (annual returns): https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/New_Home_Page/datafile/histretSP.html
- Source (1998 correction): https://www.bis.org/cgfs/Gonzalez-Hermosillo-et-al.pdf (BIS paper on contagion effects)

### Gold — Asian Crisis 1997–1998
- Gold **1997 annual return: -21.41%**
- Gold **1998 annual return: -0.83%**
- Source: https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/New_Home_Page/datafile/histretSP.html (Damodaran annual returns)

### LTCM (Long-Term Capital Management)
- **Leverage ratio at year-end 1997:** ~30:1 (debt to capital)
- **Leverage ratio by end-August 1998:** ~50:1
- **Leverage ratio by late September 1998:** estimated 130:1 to 250:1 as losses mounted
- **Total losses:** $4.6 billion in less than 4 months (1998); lost 52%+ of investor capital from start of year
- **Bailout:** September 23, 1998 — consortium of **14 banks** recapitalized LTCM with **$3.625 billion**, organized by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York (Fed did not contribute its own funds)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-Term_Capital_Management
- Source: https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/ltcm-near-failure

### Russia's 1998 Default
- **Default date:** August 17, 1998 — Russia devalued the ruble, defaulted on domestic GKO debt, and declared moratorium on foreign debt
- **Ruble devaluation:** Approximately 75% over the crisis period (from ~6 rubles/USD to ~24 rubles/USD)
- **GKO haircuts:** GKOs discounted at ~79.9%; OFZ bonds at 57–60% depending on maturity; average investor losses via novation scheme were substantial
- **Prior IMF package:** $22.6 billion (IMF + World Bank, approved July 13, 1998) — failed to prevent default
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998_Russian_financial_crisis
- Source: https://elischolar.library.yale.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=14310&context=ypfs-documents (Yale YPFS case study)

### S&P 500 Contagion Correction — Late 1998
- Peak to trough correction: **~19%** (July 17 to October 8, 1998)
- Recovery: S&P 500 hit a new high by November 27, 1998
- Fed cut rates twice (September 29 and October 15, 1998) to contain spillover
- Source: https://www.bis.org/cgfs/Gonzalez-Hermosillo-et-al.pdf

## 6. Argentina Crisis (2001–2002)

### Peso Devaluation
- **Peg ended:** Early January 2002; provisional new rate set at 1.40 pesos per USD (initial 29% devaluation from 1:1 peg)
- **Free-float low by June 2002:** approximately **3.90 pesos per USD**
- **Effective devaluation from peg:** ~75–80% at the worst point
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998%E2%80%932002_Argentine_great_depression
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corralito

### Argentine Bond Default
- **Default declared:** December 26, 2001 (largest sovereign default in history at the time)
- **Total external debt defaulted: $93 billion** (of which ~$81.8 billion was in bonds)
- **2005 restructuring haircut:** approximately **70–76.8%** of face value (NPV basis)
  - Cruces & Trebesch (2013) estimate: **76.8%** average loss
  - Bedford et al. (2005) estimate: **~70%**
  - Bondholders settled for ~30 cents on the dollar
- **76.2% participation rate** in the 2005 exchange
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentine_debt_restructuring
- Source: https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/sovereign-default-and-debt-restructuring-was-argentinas-haircut-excessive

### Argentine GDP Decline
- Real GDP fell **~28%** from peak (1998) to trough (2002)
- 2002 alone: GDP fell **~10–11%**
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998%E2%80%932002_Argentine_great_depression
- Source: https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2002/10/learning-from-argentina-crisis/

### Corralito (Bank Freeze)
- **Start date:** December 1, 2001
- **End date (peso accounts fully free):** December 2, 2002 (~13 months)
- **Restrictions:** Cash withdrawals limited to 250 pesos/week per account holder; later raised to 300 pesos/week. Electronic transactions, credit/debit cards continued.
- **Additional note:** Dollar-denominated accounts were forcibly converted to pesos ("pesification") at 1.40 ARS/USD rate — effectively a confiscation given the market rate went to 4 ARS/USD
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corralito
- Source: https://www.mondaq.com/argentina/capital-adequacybasel/19041/end-of-banking-restrictions-and-lifting-of-the-corralito

### Who Held Argentine Bonds
- Approximately **76.2% of bondholders** eventually participated in the 2005 restructuring
- Holders included international banks, institutional investors, and a large portion (~40%) of **Italian retail investors** who had been sold Argentine bonds as relatively safe products
- A significant "holdout" group (led by NML Capital/Elliott Management) litigated for over a decade
- Source: https://www.americasquarterly.org/article/why-was-argentinas-2001-default-so-contentious/
