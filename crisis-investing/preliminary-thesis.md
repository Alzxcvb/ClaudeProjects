## Executive Summary

We analyzed asset performance across 10 major economic crises spanning 2,000+ years — from the fall of Rome to the 2020 COVID crash. The central finding is that **no single asset class is a universal safe haven**. Asset performance during crises depends almost entirely on the *type* of crisis, and a portfolio optimized for one crisis type can be catastrophically wrong for another.

This document presents five preliminary theses for team discussion, each grounded in historical evidence with specific data points.

---

## Thesis 1: Gold Is Overrated as a Universal Hedge, but Underrated for Its Specific Niche

### The Narrative vs. The Evidence

The popular narrative — "buy gold when things go wrong" — is a dangerous oversimplification. Gold's track record is highly dependent on the type of crisis.

**Where gold excels (inflation/currency crises):**
- 1970s stagflation: Gold appreciated ~2,000% ($35 to $800/oz) while the Misery Index tripled
- Weimar hyperinflation (1921-23): Gold was THE asset that preserved wealth as the Reichsmark lost 99.9% of purchasing power
- Soviet collapse (1991-92): Physical gold maintained value while the ruble experienced 2,300% inflation
- Roman Empire decline: The gold Solidus (4.5g pure gold, introduced ~312 AD) remained stable in weight and purity for 700 years while silver coinage lost 99.5% of its metal content

**Where gold disappoints (deflationary panics):**
- 2008 GFC acute phase (September 2008): Gold *fell* during the initial panic as investors liquidated all assets for cash. It then recovered and gained 166% by 2011 — but the timing gap matters
- Great Depression: Gold's purchasing power increased, but primarily because everything else was falling, not because gold was actively appreciating. Roosevelt then confiscated private gold holdings in 1933
- COVID 2020: Academic studies found gold showed *negative* safe-haven properties in some markets (China, Japan), moving with stock losses rather than against them

### The Reframe

Gold is not a universal crisis hedge. Gold is a **currency confidence hedge**. It works when the crisis involves a loss of faith in fiat currency or the issuing government. It underperforms when the crisis is demand destruction or a deflationary liquidity crunch.

**Implication for the model:** Gold allocation should be weighted based on probability of inflationary vs. deflationary outcome. In the current environment (tariff-driven uncertainty, potential stagflation), gold's niche may be highly relevant.

## Thesis 2: Productive Farmland Is the Most Consistently Underappreciated Crisis Asset

### The Evidence

Farmland's crisis performance is remarkably strong and remarkably under-discussed in institutional portfolios:

- **2008 GFC:** Farmland gained ~30% while the S&P 500 fell 57% and residential real estate collapsed
- **2000-01 recession:** Farmland produced "strong, positive returns"
- **Post-Roman Empire:** Land became the fundamental store of value — the entire feudal system emerged because productive land was the only asset that continued generating survival utility (food, shelter, security) independent of monetary systems or trade networks
- **Soviet collapse:** Privatized apartments and dachas (rural properties with agricultural capacity) were among the few assets that preserved wealth

### Why Farmland Is Different from Real Estate

This distinction is critical. Farmland and urban real estate behave like different asset classes during crises:

| Factor | Farmland | Urban Real Estate |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Value driver | Farm income, commodity prices | Credit availability, speculation |
| Crisis correlation | Low/uncorrelated with equities | Highly correlated with credit cycles |
| 2008 performance | +30% | Collapsed (Manhattan -67% in Great Depression) |
| Utility in collapse | Produces food — survival asset | Requires functioning urban economy |
| Liquidity | Very low | Moderate (but freezes in crisis) |

### The Caveat

Farmland is illiquid, difficult to access for most investors, and requires specialized knowledge. It also failed during the 1981-82 recession (farm debt crisis, falling grain prices). But its structural decoupling from equity and credit markets makes it a powerful diversifier.

**Implication for the model:** Farmland or farmland-linked instruments (REITs, agricultural commodity exposure) deserve a dedicated allocation bucket, separate from general real estate.

## Thesis 3: Government Debt Is a Binary Bet on the Issuing Government's Survival

### The Split

Government bonds show the starkest divergence of any asset class — they're either the best or the worst crisis investment, with almost nothing in between.

**When sovereign debt works (government survives):**
- 2008 GFC: US Treasuries showed safe-haven behavior with negative beta to equities (-0.09 to -0.11), rising while stocks fell
- 2011 US downgrade: Despite S&P downgrading US debt, 10-year Treasury yields were *lower* a year later due to flight-to-safety
- Every modern recession where the issuing state remains stable: bonds are the classic deflation hedge

**When sovereign debt goes to zero (government doesn't survive or restructures):**
- Soviet Union (1991): Ruble-denominated government bonds became worthless — 2,300% inflation in 1992 alone
- Greece (2012): Private bondholders took a 50% haircut — €100 billion in losses
- Argentina (2001): Creditors accepted haircuts up to 75%
- 1840s US: 19 of 26 US states defaulted on their debts
- Roman Empire: Government obligations became worthless as the state fragmented
- Venezuela (2017): Default on $65 billion in external debt

### The Decision Framework

The question isn't "are bonds safe?" — it's "do you believe this government will exist and honor its debts in the relevant time frame?"

**Key variable: reserve currency status.** Countries that issue the global reserve currency (currently USD) have a structural advantage — they can print money to service debt. This doesn't prevent inflation, but it prevents outright default. Non-reserve-currency sovereigns don't have this backstop.

**Implication for the model:** Sovereign debt allocation should be explicitly conditional on regime stability assessment. US Treasuries and peripheral sovereign debt should be modeled as fundamentally different instruments, not grouped together as "bonds."

## Thesis 4: Crisis Classification Matters More Than Asset Selection

### The Core Problem

A portfolio optimized for inflation will get destroyed by deflation, and vice versa. This is not a theoretical concern — it's the central challenge of crisis hedging.

**Example:** In the 1970s stagflation, gold gained 2,000% while bonds delivered negative real returns. In the 2008 deflationary panic, bonds were the best-performing asset while gold initially fell. An investor who held the "wrong" crisis hedge would have been hurt by the very position meant to protect them.

### The Proposed Taxonomy

Our research identifies five distinct crisis types, each with a different optimal asset allocation:

| Crisis Type | Historical Examples | Best Performers | Worst Performers |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Inflationary / Stagflation** | 1970s, Weimar, current tariff risk? | Gold, commodities, farmland | Bonds, cash, long-duration assets |
| **Deflationary Panic** | Great Depression, 2008 acute phase | Reserve-currency bonds, cash | Commodities, leveraged assets |
| **Currency Collapse** | Soviet Union, Venezuela, Zimbabwe | Foreign hard currency, gold, real assets | Anything in collapsing currency |
| **Speculative Bubble Pop** | Tulips 1637, South Sea 1720, Dot-com 2000 | Cash, enterprises with real cash flow | The bubble asset, leveraged positions |
| **Regime / State Collapse** | Western Rome ~476, Soviet Union 1991 | Gold, productive land, portable skills | Government debt, urban real estate |

### The Implication

The model's primary function should be **crisis-type identification**, not asset selection. Once the crisis type is classified, the asset allocation follows somewhat mechanically. The hard part — and where the model adds value — is in the classification step.

**Open question for the team:** Should the model attempt to maintain positions across all five crisis types simultaneously (permanent all-weather hedge), or should it dynamically shift based on crisis probability assessment (tactical allocation)?

## Thesis 5: Skills, Optionality, and Geographic Diversification Are the Ultimate Tail-Risk Hedge

### The Historical Pattern

In every civilizational-scale collapse we studied, financial assets eventually failed. What survived was human capital:

- **Roman Empire collapse:** Military capability, agricultural knowledge, and the ability to provide physical protection became the basis of feudal power. Financial wealth without physical security was worthless
- **Soviet collapse:** Those with technical skills, foreign language ability, or connections to the emerging market economy adapted fastest. Apartment privatization (a legal option, not a financial asset) was one of the best moves
- **Weimar Germany:** Holders of foreign currency and those with international connections preserved wealth; domestic-only holders were wiped out

### Modern Equivalents

For ultra-high-net-worth clients, this translates to:
- **Geographic diversification:** Assets and residency options across multiple jurisdictions
- **Multiple citizenships/residency permits:** Optionality to relocate if one jurisdiction fails
- **Liquid, portable wealth:** Gold, foreign currency, assets not tied to a single legal system
- **Skills and network:** A professional network that transcends borders

### Why This Matters for the Model

This thesis isn't directly investable in the traditional sense, but it informs portfolio construction:
- Don't concentrate all assets in a single jurisdiction or currency
- Maintain some allocation to physically portable stores of value
- The option to exit a failing system is itself a valuable asset

**Implication:** The model should include a "regime optionality" layer that goes beyond traditional asset allocation.

## Preliminary Asset Ranking by Crisis Resilience

Based on cross-crisis analysis, here is our preliminary ranking of asset classes by historical resilience:

| Rank | Asset Class | Strengths | Weaknesses | Overall |
|------|-------------|-----------|------------|---------|
| 1 | **Gold** | Inflation/currency crises, 2000+ year track record | Deflation, initial panic sell-offs, confiscation risk | Best single crisis asset but not universal |
| 2 | **Productive farmland** | Uncorrelated to equities, survival utility, regime-change resilient | Illiquid, 1981-82 failure, access barriers | Most underappreciated diversifier |
| 3 | **Reserve-currency sovereign bonds** | Deflationary panics, flight-to-safety | Inflation destroys real returns, worthless if issuer fails | Excellent for demand-destruction crises |
| 4 | **Foreign hard currency** | Currency collapse, hyperinflation | Opportunity cost in stable times, access restrictions | Critical for emerging market crises |
| 5 | **Commodity producers (equities)** | Inflation hedge via real assets, dividend income | Fall with equities in acute panic | Better than physical commodities for liquidity |
| 6 | **Defensive equities** (staples, healthcare, utilities) | Milder drawdowns vs. cyclicals | Still fall 30%+ in severe panics | Cushion, not protection |
| 7 | **Art / collectibles** | Lower correlation, -27% vs -57% (stocks) in 2008 | Illiquid, subjective valuation, storage costs | Niche diversifier |
| 8 | **Physical commodities** (oil, silver, agriculture) | Strong inflation hedge | Pro-cyclical — fall in demand destruction | Timing-dependent |
| 9 | **Non-reserve sovereign bonds** | Higher yields in stable times | Haircuts of 50-75% in restructuring | High risk in the scenarios we care about |
| 10 | **Speculative / bubble assets** | High upside in the run-up | Total loss when bubble pops | Avoid during uncertainty |

## What This Means for Today (April 2025 Context)

The current environment — tariff escalation, trade war uncertainty, potential stagflation — maps most closely to a **Type 1 (Inflationary/Stagflation)** or **Type 4 (Speculative Bubble Pop)** scenario, or potentially a combination.

- If stagflation materializes: gold, commodities, and farmland are historically optimal
- If tariffs trigger demand destruction / deflation: reserve-currency bonds and cash become the play
- The ambiguity between these two outcomes is itself the core challenge

**This is the key question for our team discussion:** how do we position for an environment where the crisis type itself is uncertain?
